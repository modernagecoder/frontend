# Math catch-up program: behind is a location, not a destiny

**The 20-second answer:** a real catch-up program diagnoses backward to find solid ground, rebuilds the missing load-bearing ideas instead of re-explaining this week's homework louder, and runs two clocks at once, repairing the past while keeping the present survivable.

## The anatomy of "behind"

Math is cumulative: a missed grade 3 idea (the fraction number line) becomes grade 4 equivalence trouble, grade 5 arithmetic trouble, grade 6 ratio trouble, each looking like a new problem. The good news hides in the same structure: catching up almost never means relearning years of math. Fix the two or three load-bearing ideas and whole grade-levels reconnect, because they were disconnected, not absent.

## Why homework help fails behind children

It teaches Tuesday's topic on the same air the last hundred topics were built on. The child gets it in session, forgets by Friday, and everyone blames the child. The child was never the problem; the sequence was.

## The five stages

1. Diagnose (the free first hour): work backward down the ladder to solid ground; name the real gaps in plain language.
2. Stabilize (weeks 1-2): engineered early wins + homework triage.
3. Rebuild (the core weeks): the missing ideas taught properly, concrete first, no bell forcing us onward.
4. Reconnect: years of "forgotten" material snaps back onto the new foundation, the light-turning-on stage.
5. Catch and pass: level with the class, the same mentor keeps climbing.

## Honest timelines (at 2 classes/week)

One term behind: 4-8 weeks. About a year behind: one school term. Two-plus years: two terms, honestly. "Fine but fragile": 4-6 weeks to attach reasons to procedures. Monthly billing: commit four weeks at a time, re-decide with evidence.

## The worked shape

A 7th grader "failing proportions" traced to grade 4 fraction equivalence: 3 weeks rebuilding equivalence with models, 2 weeks re-running ratios on the new floor, then proportions took one week instead of a failed term. The presenting problem is almost never the actual gap.
