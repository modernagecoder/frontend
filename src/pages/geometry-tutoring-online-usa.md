## The problem we solve

Algebra asks "what's the answer?"; geometry asks "how do you know?" It's the first course
that asks you to prove something must be true — to lay out an argument others can follow —
instead of computing a number, and students who relied on procedures don't know where to
begin. The second hurdle is visual: geometry hides the information you need inside a figure.
We teach proof as a plain chain of "if-then" steps and train figure-reading live.

## How we teach

1. **Reframe proof as a chain of reasons** — statements, each justified by the one before.
2. **Master the toolkit of facts** — congruence criteria, angle relationships, circle theorems.
3. **Train reading the figure** — extract givens, mark diagrams, add the auxiliary line.
4. **Connect to algebra and the plane** — coordinate geometry and trig tie it all together.

## A real worked example: a two-column proof is just "if-then" steps

Prove the base angles of an isosceles triangle are equal:

- draw the bisector from A to midpoint M of BC (construction)
- AB = AC (given); BM = CM (midpoint); AM = AM (same segment)
- △ABM ≅ △ACM (SSS)
- ∠B = ∠C (matching parts of congruent triangles)

Every line is "this is true, and here's why." Once a student sees a proof is a chain of
small justified steps — not a magic ritual — the panic disappears. It's the same skill as
explaining why a piece of code is correct.

## Why a coding school teaches geometry

A geometry proof and a correct program are built the same way: **if-then logic** (the heart
of every program), **using what's given** (a proof works only from stated facts, like code
from its inputs), and **one sound step at a time** (building a proof line by line is
debugging in advance). It's the most transferable thinking in high school math.
