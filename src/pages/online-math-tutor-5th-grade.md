## Adding unlike fractions, without the butterfly.

Worked example · 2/3 + 1/4Compute 2/3 + 1/4.

**The trick version:** multiply diagonally, add, multiply the bottoms: 11/12. Fast, correct, and the child has no idea why, so 2/3 + 1/4 + 1/2 next year produces chaos.

**How our mentor teaches it:** put both on the number line. The child sees the problem immediately: thirds and fourths are different-sized steps; you cannot count steps of different sizes together. So the question becomes: is there a step size that fits both?

Thirds: 0 . . 1/3 . . 2/3 . . 1 (steps of 1/3 = 4/12)Fourths: 0 . 1/4 . 2/4 . 3/4 . 1 (steps of 1/4 = 3/12)Common step: twelfths2/3 = 8/12 1/4 = 3/12 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12

The child has just invented common denominators. From here the written method is taught at full speed, but it rests on a picture the child can rebuild whenever a strange case appears. That difference is invisible on this week's worksheet and decisive on next year's.

## Dividing by a fraction, where "makes smaller" dies.

Worked example · 3 ÷ 1/2A recipe needs 1/2 cup of flour per batch.You have 3 cups of flour.How many batches can you make?

**What the rule-follower does:** sees division, expects a smaller answer, gets 6, and quietly loses faith in either the answer or themselves. Some "fix" it to 1.5 because that feels more like division.

**How our mentor teaches it:** the question in plain words is "how many halves fit inside 3?" Draw three cups, split each into halves, count.

[cup 1: 2 halves] [cup 2: 2 halves] [cup 3: 2 halves]3 ÷ 1/2 = 6 batches

Division never meant "make smaller". It always meant "how many of these fit in that?", and with fractions the honest meaning finally shows itself. Once the child owns this picture, "flip and multiply" in grade 6 becomes a shortcut they can verify instead of a spell they must trust.

**The parent test:** ask your 5th grader why 3 ÷ 1/2 is 6. If the answer involves a rule instead of a picture, this is exactly the gap we close.

## Why 0.3 × 0.4 is 0.12, seen rather than counted.

Worked example · decimal multiplicationCompute 0.3 × 0.4.

**The ritual answer:** 3 × 4 = 12, count two decimal places, write 0.12. Correct, and utterly unexplained, which is why half the class writes 1.2 the moment the ritual slips.

**How our mentor teaches it:** the question means "three tenths of four tenths". On a hundredths grid, shade four tenths of the square, then take three tenths of that shaded strip. The overlap is the answer, and the child can count it: 12 little squares out of 100.

0.4 of the grid = 40 hundredths0.3 of those = 12 hundredths0.3 × 0.4 = 0.12 (and it SHOULD be smaller than both: a part of a part)

The size instinct is the real lesson: a part of a part must be smaller. A child carrying that instinct sanity-checks every decimal product for the rest of their life, which is worth more than any counted decimal place.

## The terms on your child's worksheets, decoded.

### Common denominator

A shared step size that lets unlike fractions be counted together. Discovered on the number line, not decreed.

### Thousandths

The third decimal place: 0.001. Place value continuing its tenfold pattern past the point.

### Powers of 10

10, 100, 1000 written as repeated multiplication. Why digits shift when you multiply by them.

### Scaling

Multiplication as resizing: times 3/2 stretches, times 2/3 shrinks. The idea that retires "multiplication makes bigger".

### Unit fraction division

Problems like 3 divided by 1/2, read as "how many halves fit in 3", the gateway to all fraction division.

### Fraction as division

3/4 literally means 3 divided by 4. The link that lets sharing problems end in fractions.

### Volume

Space filled, measured in unit cubes, versus area, which is surface covered. Built by stacking layers.

### Coordinate plane

The grid where points live as (x, y) pairs, first quadrant only in grade 5, and where algebra will later draw its lines.

### Ordered pair

(4, 2): four across, two up, and the order is the whole point.

### Order of operations

The grouping rules that make 3 + 4 x 2 equal 11, taught as why grouping exists rather than as a mnemonic.

### Numerical expression

A calculation written in symbols, like 2 x (8 + 7), the on-ramp to algebra.

### Benchmark estimation

Rounding a calculation to friendly numbers first, so absurd answers get caught before they are written.
