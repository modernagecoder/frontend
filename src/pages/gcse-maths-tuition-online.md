## The problem we solve

Covering every topic isn't the same as using any of them under pressure. The harder marks
come from problem-solving questions that combine topics and look nothing like the textbook
example; a student who learned each topic as a separate procedure has no way in. The other
grade-killer is method marks — lost by not showing working, or by an old algebra/fractions
gap surfacing inside a bigger question. We diagnose where marks leak, repair the foundations
the exam keeps testing, teach the reasoning the problem-solving questions need, and drill
past-paper technique.

## A real worked example: a higher-tier worded question

"The product of two consecutive integers is 156. Find them." The skill is turning words into
algebra:

- two consecutive integers → n and n+1; product 156 → n(n+1) = 156
- n² + n − 156 = 0 → (n + 13)(n − 12) = 0
- n = 12 → the integers are **12 and 13** (check: 12 × 13 = 156)

Nothing is beyond the specification — the difficulty was translating words into an equation
and recognising a quadratic. That translation skill separates a grade 5 from a grade 7.

## Why a coding school teaches GCSE maths

Turning a worded problem into maths is what programming trains: **translate the problem**
(vague requirement into precise logic), **break it into steps** (decomposition), and **check
your answer** (debugging). Our students tend to handle the problem-solving parts that leave
others stuck.
