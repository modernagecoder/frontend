The obsession, redirected

The hours are being spent either way. The question is which side of the screen.

Parents rarely need convincing that their child loves Roblox; the battle is usually about how much. Here is the reframe that changes the conversation: Roblox is not just a game, it is the largest game-creation platform on earth, and every experience your child plays was built by someone in Roblox Studio, a free, professional-grade tool, scripted in Luau, a real programming language descended from Lua, which is used across the games industry.

A child who crosses from player to builder gets something remarkable: the strongest motivation engine in their life pointed at real programming. Variables stop being abstract when they hold a coin count. Functions matter when they open your game's door. Debugging is suddenly worth it when it is your obby that is broken.

The catch is the same as everywhere in kids' coding: most "Roblox classes" are recipe-following, paste this script, drag that model, and most self-taught kids assemble games from free models without understanding a line. The result looks like progress and teaches almost nothing.

Our mentors teach the programming underneath, live, with your child's hands on the keyboard. Scripts are written, not pasted; every line gets a "what do you think this does?" before it runs. And because our school teaches coding from ages 6 to 18, Roblox is a road here too: Luau's variables, loops and functions transfer directly to Python and beyond when your child is ready. Watch the method work

Seven lines of Luau, and the door obeys your child.

From a real early lesson · the touch-activated door
local door = script.Parent

local function openDoor(otherPart)
  door.Transparency = 0.8
  door.CanCollide = false
end

door.Touched:Connect(openDoor)

What the child sees: they walk their avatar into a solid door, and it turns to glass and lets them through. Their code did that.

What the child just used: a variable holding an object, a function they defined, properties they changed with code, and an event connecting the physical world to their logic. Four foundation ideas, seven lines, week two.

The mentor's questions do the teaching: "why 0.8 and not 0?" "what happens if we skip CanCollide?" "how would we close it again after three seconds?" That last one leads straight to task scheduling, and the child asks for it themselves, because it is their door. Motivation this strong cannot be manufactured; it can only be pointed in the right direction.

One more, further down the road

The leaderboard: where variables grow up.

Month 4-6 · real game systems
local Players = game:GetService("Players")

Players.PlayerAdded:Connect(function(player)
  local stats = Instance.new("Folder", player)
  stats.Name = "leaderstats"
  local coins = Instance.new("IntValue", stats)
  coins.Name = "Coins"
  coins.Value = 0
end)

What the child sees: every player who joins their game now has a live coin counter on the leaderboard, the feature every "real" Roblox game has.

Underneath, the child is now handling services, player events, object creation at runtime and data structure, the same concepts a professional games programmer uses daily, arrived at because their tycoon needed coins. From here the road runs to touch-collected pickups, saving data between sessions, and multi-script architectures. And when this child later meets Python or JavaScript, they will recognize everything: objects, events, functions, state. The costume changes; the ideas are already theirs.

Parent questions

Everything parents ask about Roblox classes.

What age is right for Roblox coding classes?

Ages 9-14 is the sweet spot: old enough to type and reason about scripts, young enough that the motivation engine is at maximum. Ages 6-8 usually thrive on our Scratch track first and arrive in Studio ready. The free demo doubles as a placement either way.

Is Roblox scripting real programming?

Genuinely, yes. Roblox experiences are scripted in Luau, a typed dialect of Lua, a language family used across the professional games industry. Your child writes variables, functions, events, conditionals and loops in real text code. The concepts transfer directly to Python, JavaScript and beyond.

Is it safe? Roblox has a reputation.

Two different things get mixed up here. The public Roblox platform has the risks any social game has, which is why we brief parents on account controls, chat settings and spending locks at the start. Our classes happen in Roblox Studio, the development tool: no public servers, no strangers, just your child, the mentor and the build.

My child just plays Roblox all day. Will this reduce screen time?

It changes what the screen time is, which parents tell us matters more. Building is active, effortful and creative where playing is consumptive, and most children voluntarily trade play hours for build hours once their own game exists. The battles tend to soften within a month.

Can my child really make money on Roblox?

We answer this honestly because the hype does not. A small number of teen developers earn real money; the overwhelming majority do not, and chasing Robux is the wrong reason to learn. We teach the economy as literacy, how monetization works, what the marketplace rewards, while aiming at the payoff that is guaranteed: a child who can actually program.

Does my child need to be good at Roblox to start?

No. Playing skill and building skill are unrelated; we have taught brilliant builders who are mediocre at obbies. Curiosity about how games work is the only prerequisite that matters.

Free models and AI tools can build games now. Why learn scripting?

Because assembly without understanding hits a ceiling fast: the moment your child wants the door to behave differently, they need the code. We let children use models and modern tools the way professionals do, on top of understanding, never instead of it. That is also our house rule for pasted scripts.

What does it cost?

1-on-1 is $100 a month and small group is $40 a month, both with 8 live one-hour classes (2 per week) and recordings included. No registration fee, no contract. US kids-coding programs typically run $175 to $350 a month for less contact time.

What equipment does my child need?

A computer (Windows or Mac) that runs Roblox Studio, which is free, plus stable internet and a mouse. Studio does not run on tablets or phones, which is honestly part of its value: it moves your child from consumption hardware to creation hardware.

Is this affiliated with Roblox Corporation?

No. Roblox and Roblox Studio are trademarks of Roblox Corporation; we are an independent school that teaches programming using their free tools, and we say so plainly.

Can we try before paying anything?

Yes. Every child starts with a free live demo class that doubles as the placement, they will script something real in Studio inside that hour, and nobody asks for a card. The promise is written on our guarantee page.
