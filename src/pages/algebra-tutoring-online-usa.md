## The problem we solve

Algebra is the first math you can't fake. For years, math is concrete; then algebra asks
students to reason about a letter standing for an unknown, and the ground shifts. Drilling
the same problem type backfires — it reinforces copying steps, so a rephrased test question
loses the points. Algebra also exposes old cracks in fractions and negatives. We repair the
buried gaps and teach the abstraction itself, so your child reasons through algebra instead
of guessing.

## A real worked example: why "do the same to both sides" works

An equation is a claim that two things are equal — a balanced scale. 3x + 4 = 19 says both
pans weigh the same; do the identical thing to both and they stay balanced:

- 3x + 4 = 19 (balanced)
- subtract 4 from both pans → 3x = 15 (still balanced)
- divide both pans by 3 → x = 5 (still balanced)

"The same to both sides" isn't a rule to obey — it's the obvious way to keep a balance true
while simplifying it. Students stop changing only one side and can reason about equations
they've never met. This idea underpins all of Algebra 1 and 2.

## Why a coding school teaches algebra

Algebra is where math and code first speak the same language: **variables** (a name for a
value that can change), **functions** (input in, output out), and **logical equivalence**
(rearranging while preserving truth, like refactoring code). A student who understands
variables and functions walks into coding already fluent in its core ideas.
