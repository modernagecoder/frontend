## The problem we solve

Most children don't dislike maths — they dislike being hurried past the bit that would have
made it make sense. A method shown for fractions works on the worksheet, the class moves on,
and a year later it collapses inside a bigger problem, taking confidence with it. That's how
a curious six-year-old becomes a ten-year-old who says they're "rubbish at maths". One child,
one tutor, and the freedom to stop on the exact sentence where it stopped making sense.

## How we teach

1. **Start from what they already know** — new maths as the next step, not a cliff.
2. **Build the idea they can see and touch** — drawings, number lines and objects before any rule.
3. **Let them explain it back** — a child who can say *why* owns the idea.
4. **Practise to fluency, then connect forward** — so nothing feels like a dead end.

## A real worked example: number bonds make mental maths fast

10 is easy to work with, so we teach children to make a 10 on the way. For 7 + 8:

- split the 7 into 3 + 4
- give the 3 to the 8 → 8 + 3 = 10
- now 10 + 4 = **15**

Knowing the pairs that make 10 means a child reasons quickly from a handful of friendly facts
instead of memorising a hundred separate sums. It's faster, never leaves them stuck, and
builds the place-value thinking all of primary maths rests on.

## Why a coding school teaches maths

The thinking that builds a strong coder builds a strong mathematician: **break the big into
the small** (decomposition), **spot the pattern** (the instinct behind a loop), and **check
your own work** (debugging). A child who learns maths as reasoning walks into coding and
science already knowing how to work things out.
