The 20-second answer

A real math catch-up program does three things a regular tutor rarely does: diagnoses backward down the grade ladder to find where solid ground actually is; rebuilds the missing ideas properly instead of re-explaining this week's homework louder; and runs two clocks at once, repairing the past while keeping your child from drowning in the present. That is what we do: 8 live one-hour classes a month with one dedicated mentor, 1-on-1 for $150 a month or small group for $100, free diagnostic demo first.

The anatomy of "behind"

Gaps compound. That is the bad news and the good news.

Math is the most cumulative subject in school. A child who missed the fraction number line in grade 3 will struggle with equivalence in grade 4, arithmetic in grade 5, ratios in grade 6 and equations in grade 7, and at every stage the struggle will look like a new problem. Parents get told "she needs to focus on proportions" when the truth is a grade 3 idea was never built. That is how a small gap becomes three years of quiet suffering.

The good news hides in the same structure: because everything traces back, catching up almost never means relearning three years of math. It means finding the two or three load-bearing ideas that are actually missing, rebuilding those, and watching whole grade-levels of "forgotten" material snap back into place, because it was never truly missing, just disconnected.

This is why generic homework help so often fails a behind child: it teaches Tuesday's topic on top of the same air the last hundred topics were built on. The child "gets it" in the session, forgets by Friday, and everyone concludes the child is the problem. The child was never the problem. The sequence was.

Catching up is also emotional work. By the time a family searches for a program like this, the child has usually spent months collecting evidence that they are "just not a math person." Our mentors treat that belief as the first gap to repair: early, genuine wins, engineered on purpose, because a child who believes movement is possible moves twice as fast.

How catching up actually works

Five stages, two clocks, one mentor.

The hard part of catching up is that school does not pause while you repair. We run both clocks deliberately: most of each week rebuilds the foundation, a protected slice keeps your child functional in current classwork, and the balance shifts as the foundation rises to meet the present.

Diagnose · the free first hour

We work backward down the grade ladder until we find solid ground, and name the two or three load-bearing ideas that are actually missing. You get the map in plain language.

Stabilize · weeks 1-2

Early wins on nearly-solid topics rebuild the belief that movement is possible, while triage keeps current homework survivable.

Rebuild · the core weeks

The missing ideas get taught properly, concrete first, at your child's pace, with no bell forcing us onward before they hold.

Reconnect · watching it snap back

Years of "forgotten" material get reattached to the new foundation, fast, because it was disconnected rather than absent. This is the stage parents describe as the light turning on.

Catch and pass · the goal was never "average"

Once level with the class, the same mentor keeps going. Children who catch up properly usually keep climbing, because they now own tools their classmates rent.

Watch the method work

A real catch-up shape: the 7th grader whose problem lived in grade 4.

Worked example · the diagnostic ladder
"My son is failing 7th grade math. His teacher says he struggles with proportions.
We tried a tutor for proportions. It did not stick."

Why the proportion tutoring failed: proportions are built on fraction equivalence. Test it: can he explain why 3/4 equals 6/8, with a picture? He cannot; he multiplies top and bottom because "that is the rule". One rung further down: can he place 3/4 and 6/8 on a number line? Shaky. There is the real floor, and it is a grade 4 idea.

Presenting problem:  grade 7 proportions
Actual gap:         grade 4 fraction equivalence (+ number line)
Plan:  3 weeks rebuilding equivalence with models
       2 weeks re-running ratios and unit rates ON the new floor
       then proportions, which now take one week instead of a failed term

The point is not this particular boy. The point is the shape: the presenting problem is almost never the actual gap, and teaching to the presenting problem is why so much tutoring money buys so little movement. Diagnose first. It is why our first hour is free.

Parent questions

Everything worried parents ask us, answered straight.

Is my child just not a math person?

No. In hundreds of catch-up journeys we have not met a child who could not learn math when the missing foundations were found and rebuilt at their pace. "Not a math person" is what a sequence failure feels like from the inside. The belief itself is the first thing we repair, with engineered early wins, because a child who believes movement is possible moves twice as fast.

How far behind is too far to catch up?

We have not found the line yet. Deeper gaps take longer, two years behind honestly needs two terms, but the cumulative structure of math is exactly why catch-up works: fix the load-bearing ideas and whole grade-levels reconnect. The only losing move is waiting another year.

Will schoolwork suffer while you rebuild old material?

This is the two-clock problem, and we run both clocks on purpose: most of each week rebuilds the foundation, a protected slice keeps current homework survivable, and the balance shifts as the foundation rises. Parents usually see current grades stabilize within weeks, then climb as the rebuild reaches the present.

How is this different from a regular tutor?

A regular tutor teaches this week's topic. A catch-up program diagnoses backward down the grade ladder, rebuilds the actual missing ideas, and reconnects everything on top, with one mentor tracking the whole arc. It is the difference between patching a wall and fixing the foundation the wall keeps cracking over.

How will you handle my child's confidence?

Deliberately. The first weeks are engineered for wins on nearly-solid ground, mistakes are treated as information rather than verdicts, and the child explains ideas back in their own words until they hear themselves being capable. Parents tell us in our reviews about the transformation in confidence, and we consider it half the job.

How long will it take, honestly?

After the diagnostic we will tell you which shape your child is: one term behind usually means 4 to 8 weeks; a year behind, one school term; two-plus years, two terms. Anyone quoting timelines before diagnosing is guessing. Monthly billing means you re-decide every four weeks with evidence.
