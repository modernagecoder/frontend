## The problem we solve

CE maths, especially at 13+ Level 3 and scholarship, is pitched well above the national
curriculum for the same age. It expects fluent technique *and* the confidence to work through
long, multi-step problems that don't announce their method. A capable child drilled only on
standard questions can freeze on a winding CE problem. Prep schools prepare well, but a class
can't always give the depth, pace and attention the harder levels demand. One-to-one work
builds genuine understanding and problem-solving, pitched to the exact level and target
schools.

## A real worked example: a 13+ multi-step problem

"A recipe for 6 needs 450g flour and 300ml milk. For 10 people, with only 700g flour, how
much more flour and how much milk?" Plan before calculating — find one person's share, scale,
then answer each part:

- flour per person = 450 ÷ 6 = 75g → for 10 = 750g → needs **50g more**
- milk per person = 300 ÷ 6 = 50ml → for 10 = **500ml**

The move is "find one person's share, then scale", tackling each part in order. We teach
children to slow down, plan the route and label each step, so a long CE question becomes a
series of small, calm calculations.

## Why a coding school teaches Common Entrance maths

Planning a multi-step problem is how a programmer thinks: **plan before you compute**, **break
into sub-problems**, and **check each step**. Children who learn this arrive at senior school
thinking like genuine problem-solvers.
