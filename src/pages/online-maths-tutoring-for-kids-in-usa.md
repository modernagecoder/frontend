Why so many kids stall

## Most kids don't hate math. They hate being asked to memorize things nobody explained.

A child is told to "flip and multiply" to divide fractions. It works on the worksheet, so everyone moves on. But the child has no idea what just happened — and a year later, when fractions show up inside a bigger problem, the trick falls apart and takes their confidence with it.

This is how a bright six-year-old slowly becomes an eleven-year-old who says "I'm just not a math person." It's almost never true. What broke was the explanation, not the child.

Classrooms of thirty can't always slow down for the one question that would have fixed everything. Math apps are worse — they reward the right tap but can't tell whether a child understood or just guessed.

We do the opposite. One child, one mentor, and the freedom to stop on the exact sentence where it stopped making sense. That is the whole job.

How we teach

## Understanding first. The answer comes after.

Every lesson follows the same honest shape, whether your child is counting or carrying.

### Start from something they already know

We anchor each new idea to one your child has already understood. New math should feel like the next step on a path, not a cliff.

### Build the idea with their hands and eyes

We use drawings, number lines, and objects on the whiteboard so the idea is something a child can see and move — before it ever becomes a written rule.

### Let them explain it back

A child who can say *why* in their own words actually owns the idea. We ask "how do you know?" far more than "what's the answer?"

### Practice until it's automatic — then connect it forward

Only once the reasoning is solid do we build fluency, and then we show where this idea is going next, so nothing feels like a dead end.

See it for yourself

## "Borrowing" in subtraction — taught the way we'd teach your child.

Worked example · grade 2–3

**The problem most kids get told:** "To do 52 − 27, you can't take 7 from 2, so cross out the 5, make it a 4, and put a little 1 in front of the 2." The child obeys, gets 25, and learns nothing about *why*.

**How we do it.** A number isn't just digits in boxes — it's a pile of tens and ones. 52 is five tens and two ones. We can't hand over 7 ones when we only have 2, so we do something completely ordinary: we break open one of the tens.

52 = 5 tens + 2 onesopen one ten → 4 tens + 12 onesnow take away 27 → (4 − 2) tens + (12 − 7) ones = 2 tens + 5 ones = **25**

The child sees that "borrowing" was never a magic trick — it was just trading one ten for ten ones, the same way you'd break a $10 bill into ten $1 bills to pay for something. Once they've seen it, they never forget how to do it, and they understand place value, which is the foundation of everything that comes after.

Why a coding school teaches math

## The thinking that builds a strong coder is the same thinking that builds a strong mathematician.

### Break the big into the small

A programmer splits a hard task into tiny steps. So does a child solving "how many wheels on 8 tricycles?" Decomposition is the same muscle.

### Find the pattern

Spotting that 3, 6, 9, 12 jumps by three is the same instinct that lets a coder write a loop instead of typing the same line over and over.

### Check your own work

"Does this answer make sense?" is debugging. We teach kids to test their thinking, not just trust the first number they wrote.

We are Modern Age Coders. We've taught children to think like builders for years, and math is where that thinking starts. A child who learns math as reasoning — not recipes — walks into coding, science, and every future "hard subject" already knowing how to figure things out.

What we cover

## The full elementary path, grade by grade.

Mapped to the Common Core strands your child's school uses — but always taught for understanding first.

### Number & place value

Counting and one-to-one matching, reading and writing numbers, what each digit really means, comparing and ordering, rounding — the bedrock that makes everything else possible.

### The four operations

Addition and subtraction with regrouping, multiplication as repeated groups, division as fair sharing, and fact fluency built on understanding rather than flashcard panic.

### Fractions & decimals

Fractions as real quantities you can picture, equivalence, adding and comparing, and the bridge to decimals and percentages — the topic most kids fear, made concrete.

### Word problems & reasoning

Reading a problem, deciding what it's actually asking, choosing an operation, and checking the answer is sensible. This is where math meets real thinking.

### Measurement, data & geometry

Length, time, money, area; reading and making simple graphs; shapes, symmetry and early spatial reasoning.

### Confidence & habits

How to stay calm with a hard problem, how to show working clearly, and how to treat a wrong answer as information instead of failure.
