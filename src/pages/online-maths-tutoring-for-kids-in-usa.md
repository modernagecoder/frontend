## The problem we solve

Most kids don't hate math — they hate being asked to memorize steps nobody explained. A
child told to "flip and multiply" with no reason behind it does fine on the worksheet, then
falls apart a year later when the trick meets a bigger problem. That is how a bright
six-year-old becomes an eleven-year-old who says "I'm not a math person." It's almost never
true. What broke was the explanation, not the child.

## How we teach

1. **Start from something they already know** — new math should feel like the next step, not a cliff.
2. **Build the idea with hands and eyes** — drawings, number lines and objects on a shared whiteboard before any written rule.
3. **Let them explain it back** — a child who can say *why* in their own words owns the idea.
4. **Practice to fluency, then connect it forward** — so nothing feels like a dead end.

## A real worked example: why "borrowing" works

For 52 − 27, instead of "cross out the 5, make a little 1," we show that 52 is five tens and
two ones. We can't give away 7 ones from only 2, so we break open one ten:

- 52 = 5 tens + 2 ones
- open one ten → 4 tens + 12 ones
- take away 27 → 2 tens + 5 ones = **25**

"Borrowing" was never magic — it's trading one ten for ten ones, like breaking a $10 bill
into ten $1 bills. The child understands place value, the foundation of everything after.

## Why a coding school teaches math

The thinking that builds a strong coder is the same thinking that builds a strong
mathematician: **break big problems into small steps** (decomposition), **find the pattern**
(the instinct behind a loop), and **check your own work** (debugging). A child who learns
math as reasoning rather than recipes walks into coding and science already knowing how to
figure things out.
