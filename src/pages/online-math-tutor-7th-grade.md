## Proportionality is the hinge every later course swings on.

Ask a math teacher which single skill predicts algebra success and most will give the same answer: proportional reasoning. Slope is a proportion. Similar triangles are proportions. Unit conversion, percent change, probability, direct variation, trig ratios years later: all the same mental move. Grade 7 is the year that move either becomes reasoning or hardens into "set up the two fractions and cross-multiply", a ritual that dissolves the moment problems arrive in words.

The second engine is rational-number arithmetic. Grade 7 finishes the number system: adding, subtracting, multiplying and dividing with negatives and fractions together. The sign rules look small, but every algebra error chain traces back through them. A child who "sort of knows" that negative times negative is positive, without any idea why, is one distracted moment from minus signs scattering everywhere.

And grade 7 is where tracking quietly happens. Many US districts decide algebra-in-grade-8 placement on grade 7 performance, which means this year's understanding directly shapes the high-school transcript, AP timeline and, bluntly, college options. ## The discount-plus-tax problem, solved like an adult.

Worked example · percent multipliersA $60 hoodie is 25% off. Sales tax is 8%.What do you actually pay?

**The grade-school route:** find 25% of 60, subtract, find 8% of that, add. Four steps, three chances to slip, and no reusable idea.

**How our mentor teaches it:** percent change is multiplication. "25% off" means you pay 75%, one multiplier: × 0.75. "Plus 8% tax" means × 1.08. Chain them.

60 × 0.75 = 45 (the sale price)45 × 1.08 = 48.60 (with tax)Or in one line: 60 × 0.75 × 1.08 = $48.60

Now the payoffs cascade. Reverse problems become divisions: "paid $48.60 with 8% tax, what was the sticker?" is ÷ 1.08. Repeated growth in grade 8, compound interest in high school, exponential functions later: all just multipliers chained. One idea, learned once, working for years.

**The parent test:** ask your 7th grader why 25% off means times 0.75. A reason means understanding; silence means ritual.

## Why negative times negative is positive, with a proof a 12-year-old owns.

Worked example · deriving the sign ruleWhat is (-3) × (-4), and why?

**The chant version:** "negative times negative is positive, so 12." Fine, until the same chant gets applied to -3 + (-4) and produces +7. Chants do not know which operation they belong to.

**How our mentor teaches it:** build the pattern and let the child extend it. Start with what they trust:

3 × (-4) = -122 × (-4) = -81 × (-4) = -40 × (-4) = 0-1 × (-4) = ? (each step ADDS 4... so +4)-2 × (-4) = +8-3 × (-4) = +12

The child discovers the rule by continuing a pattern they verified themselves. Now it has a proof attached, it cannot leak into addition, and when algebra floods the page with minus signs next year, this student places them with confidence while the chanters guess. ## The six grade 7 misconceptions we fix most often.

### "Two negatives make a positive" applied to addition

-3 + (-4) answered as +7. The chant belongs to multiplication only; the number line keeps addition honest.

### "Percent of the new price, percent of the old, same thing"

A 20% drop then 20% rise does not return home. Multipliers, x0.8 then x1.2, make the asymmetry visible.

### "Flip the inequality whenever a negative appears"

Over-applied ritual. Built properly from "multiplying by -1 reverses order on the line", it fires only when it should.

### "Proportional means it goes up"

Any increasing table gets called proportional. The test, straight line through the origin, constant ratio, gets installed with counterexamples.

### "2 pi r and pi r squared are interchangeable"

Distance around versus space inside. Measuring real circles until pi appears makes the two formulas unswappable.

### "Probability 1/2 because it happens or it doesn't"

The two-outcomes fallacy. Counting equally likely outcomes with lists and trees rebuilds chance from the ground.

## The two-step equation, solved by balance instead of moves.

Worked example · 3x + 7 = 22Solve: 3x + 7 = 22

**The moves version:** "move the 7 over and flip its sign, then move the 3 under." Works until 7 - 3x = 22, where the memorized moves produce chaos.

**How our mentor teaches it:** the equation is a balanced scale. Three mystery boxes and 7 ounces on the left, 22 ounces on the right. What legal moves keep it balanced?

3x + 7 = 22Remove 7 from BOTH pans: 3x = 15Split BOTH pans into 3: x = 5Check: 3(5) + 7 = 22 ✓

"Do the same to both sides" stops being a rule and becomes physics. Fractional coefficients, negatives, variables on both sides in grade 8: all just more of the same legal moves. And the check at the end builds the self-verifying habit that algebra grades quietly reward.

## The terms on your child's worksheets, decoded.

### Proportional relationship

Two quantities with a constant ratio: double one, the other doubles. Graphs as a straight line through the origin.

### Constant of proportionality

The k in y = kx: the unit rate wearing algebra clothes, and slope's direct ancestor.

### Rational number

Any number writable as a fraction, positive or negative, including decimals that end or repeat.

### Additive inverse

The opposite that sums to zero: -3 and 3. Why subtraction is adding the opposite.

### Percent multiplier

The one-step version of a percent change: 25% off is x0.75, 8% tax is x1.08. Chainable and reversible.

### Markup and markdown

Percent increase and decrease in shop language, computed with multipliers, not four-step rituals.

### Simple interest

I = prt: principal times rate times time, percent reasoning meeting money.

### Like terms

Terms with identical variable parts: 3x and 5x combine; 3x and 3x squared do not.

### Vertical angles

The opposite angles where two lines cross, always equal, and the seed of angle-chasing proofs.

### Circumference

Distance around a circle: 2 pi r. Discovered by measuring, not memorizing, so it never swaps with area.

### Sample vs population

The part you measured versus the whole you care about, and why random sampling is the bridge.

### Compound event

Two or more events together, counted with lists, tables and tree diagrams before any formula.
