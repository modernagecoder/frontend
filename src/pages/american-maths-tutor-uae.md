## Why a system's solution is where the two lines cross.

Worked example · algebra & the SAT

**What gets memorised:** students learn to "solve a system" by substitution or elimination as a procedure, with no picture of what the answer represents — so a graph-based SAT question about the same system throws them.

**How we do it.** Every linear equation is a line — a picture of all the (x, y) pairs that make it true. Two equations are two lines. The one point that satisfies *both* equations is the one point that lies on *both* lines: where they cross.

y = 2x + 1 (one line)y = −x + 4 (another line)they cross where both are true: 2x + 1 = −x + 4 → 3x = 3 → x = 1, y = 3solution = the crossing point **(1, 3)**

Now "solving a system" and "where do the lines intersect" are the same question — and a student sees why parallel lines (same slope) give no solution, and identical lines give infinitely many. That single picture connects the algebra a student does for homework, the graph questions on the Digital SAT, and the systems they'll meet in AP. Understand it once; use it everywhere.
