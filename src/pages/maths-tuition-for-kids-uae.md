## The problem we solve

Most children don't dislike maths — they dislike being hurried past the bit that would make
it make sense. In large, fast-moving classes a method gets shown, works on the worksheet, and
the class moves on; a year later it collapses and takes confidence with it. One child, one
curriculum-matched tutor, and the freedom to stop on the exact sentence where it stopped
making sense.

## How we teach

1. **Start from what they know** — new maths as the next step, not a cliff.
2. **Build it so they can see it** — drawings, number lines and objects before any rule.
3. **Let them explain it back** — a child who can say *why* owns the idea.
4. **Practise, then connect forward** — so nothing feels like a dead end.

## A real worked example: why 3 × 4 = 4 × 3

Draw 3 rows of 4 dots (= 12). Turn the same grid sideways — now it's 4 rows of 3 (still 12).
The dots never moved:

- 3 × 4 = 12 and 4 × 3 = 12 — same dots, same total

Multiplication is "rows of dots," and turning the grid can't change how many there are, so
every times-table fact comes with a free twin. That halves what a child must memorise and
shows maths has reasons.
