THE NEIGHBOURHOOD

Uluberia: ambitious children, no door to walk through

Uluberia is the subdivisional headquarters of southern Howrah — a busy Bengali municipal town strung along the Howrah-Kharagpur railway line, where the Hooghly bends past the ferry ghat to Budge Budge and NH-16 (Mumbai Road) cuts straight through the heart of it. Its economy runs on trade, jute, the light industry clustered around Bauria, and the farms that ring the town. The families here are, more often than not, first-generation aspirants — parents in trade and agriculture who want their children pointed firmly at the IT economy, even when no one at home has written a line of code.

And that is exactly where Uluberia has been short-changed. Serious offline computer-applications or coding tuition simply does not exist in the town in any real form. To reach a genuine coding tutor, a child would have to lose sixty to ninety minutes a day to NH-16 truck traffic, the crush at Uluberia bus stand, or a slow Howrah-bound local — time no school student can spare and no working parent can chaperone. The result is a town full of capable, ambitious children with no real door to walk through.

Live online is that door. Because every batch runs in real time over the internet, an Uluberia child off Station Road or out near Birshibpur joins the exact same small batch, the same full-time engineer, and the same project-based curriculum as a student in Salt Lake or Ballygunge — and does it from home, with the entire commute erased. Distance, here, is genuinely meaningless. The only thing that travels is the lesson.
