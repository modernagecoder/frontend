Why KS2 is the moment that matters

KS2 is where the maths gaps that haunt secondary school quietly form.

KS2 is when maths gets serious — long division, fractions, multi-step problems. It's also when a class of thirty can't slow down for the child who didn't quite get place value, so they're handed methods to copy and moved along. The method works on the worksheet; the understanding never forms.

Those small unfilled gaps don't show up much in primary school. They surface brutally in Year 7, when secondary maths assumes a KS2 foundation that isn't fully there.

And the Year 6 SATs add pressure that can push schools and parents toward narrow drilling — which boosts a test score briefly but leaves the same gaps underneath.

We do it properly. We build genuine understanding of every KS2 idea, which both lifts SATs performance and gives your child the solid foundation that makes Year 7 and beyond far easier. One tutor, your child's pace, no gaps left behind.

Long division — why the method works, not just how.

Worked example · Year 5–6

What often happens: children are taught the long-division ritual — "divide, multiply, subtract, bring down" — as steps to copy. They follow it, make small slips, and have no idea what the digits actually mean, so they can't spot when an answer is wrong.

How we do it. Division is just sharing into equal groups, done one place value at a time. For 432 ÷ 3, we share out the hundreds, then the tens, then the ones — exactly what the written method records:

432 ÷ 3:  share 4 hundreds into 3 groups → 1 each, 1 hundred left over
that leftover 100 becomes 10 tens, joining the 3 tens = 13 tens
share 13 tens into 3 → 4 each, 1 ten left over
that leftover 10 becomes 10 ones, joining the 2 ones = 12 ones
share 12 ones into 3 → 4 each
answer: 1 hundred, 4 tens, 4 ones = 144

The "bring down" step a child once copied blindly is just carrying a leftover into the next place value — exactly like sharing out money. Once a child sees this, long division stops being a fragile ritual they get wrong under pressure, and becomes something they understand and can check. That's the difference between a SATs score that holds up and one that's a coin-flip on the day.
