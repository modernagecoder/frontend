## The problem we solve

KS3 is the quiet danger zone. Maths turns abstract — real algebra, negative numbers, formal
ratio — and because there's no high-stakes exam at the end, a child who's quietly lost can
drift for three years unnoticed. Then GCSE arrives assuming all of KS3 is rock-solid, and the
invisible gaps become a crisis at 15. KS3 is also often taught as a checklist with little
time to make the abstraction click. We treat KS3 as the foundation it is — making new ideas
intuitive and fixing primary gaps underneath.

## A real worked example: why a negative times a negative is positive

Watch the pattern as the second number counts down:

- −3 × 2 = −6; −3 × 1 = −3; −3 × 0 = 0
- each answer goes UP by 3, so −3 × (−1) = +3, −3 × (−2) = +6

To keep that steady pattern unbroken past zero, −3 × (−1) *must* be +3. It's not an arbitrary
rule — it's the only answer that keeps the number system consistent. The student also meets a
powerful idea: maths rules are forced by consistency, not invented — exactly the mindset GCSE
rewards.

## Why a coding school teaches KS3 maths

KS3 is where maths and code start speaking the same language: **variables** (the letters in
algebra = variables in code), **rules from consistency** (predictable behaviour), and
**step-by-step thinking**. Students who learn KS3 maths properly often take to programming
naturally.
